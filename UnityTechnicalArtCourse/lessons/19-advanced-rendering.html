Lesson 19: Advanced Rendering Techniques

Polishing the Visual Experience

Our final lesson covers the "special sauce" of technical art: manipulating buffers and lighting systems to solve problems that standard pipelines can't handle. In a browser-based geospatial app, these techniques aren't just for beauty—they're for clarity and performance.

Shadows at Massive Scale

Standard Unity shadow cascades typically cover 50 to 100 meters. But at VRify, you might be looking at a mining pit that covers 10 square kilometers. If you set your shadow distance to 10km, your shadow map resolution will spread so thin that the shadows disappear or turn into a flickering mess.

The Scale Dilemma

  • Perspective Aliasing: Shadow maps are projections. The larger the area they cover, the lower the "pixels per meter" of shadow.
  • Floating Point Precision: At huge distances from the camera, Z-fighting occurs in the shadow map, causing "shadow acne."

Better Solutions:

1. Runtime Shadow Distance Modification

Don't stick to one setting. Detect the camera's zoom level. If zoomed in on a tractor, set distance to 50m. If looking at the whole site, set it to 10km (or disable it and use a different technique).

2. Top-Down Terrain Shadow Mapping

Instead of real-time directional shadows (which calculate every frame), render a single top-down depth map of the terrain once. Use this as a "fake" shadow map in your shader. It's essentially persistent ambient occlusion for the ground.

3. Contact Shadows (Screen Space)

For fine details near the camera, use Screen Space Shadows. They look better than cascades for small gaps and don't care about the global shadow distance setting.

Stencil Buffer Mastery

The **Stencil Buffer** is like an invisible 8-bit mask shared with the Depth Buffer. You can "mark" pixels as they render, and then other objects can choose to render only where the mask matches.

Example: The Boundary Desaturator

You want everything inside the mining lease to be in color, and everything outside to be black and white.

  1. Render an invisible "Boundary Mesh" first, writing 1 to the Stencil buffer.
  2. Run a fullscreen Render Feature pass (Lesson 18).
  3. In that pass shader: if (stencilValue != 1) { color = grayscale(color); }

Example: Portals

Looking "inside" a buried pipe from the surface. Use the stencil to punch a hole through the terrain's rendering logic.

Depth Buffer Tricks

The Depth (Z) Buffer tells us how far away each pixel is. We can "lie" to it to create effects.

  • X-Ray Effects: Set ZTest Greater on an outline shader. This means the outline only draws *if it is hidden* by something else. This lets you see machinery through mountains.
  • Soft Particles: Check the depth of the background in your shader. If pixelDepth - backgroundDepth is small, fade the alpha. This prevents hard "cutting" lines where clouds hit the ground.

Camera Stacking

In URP, you can stack multiple cameras on a single target. This is essential for compositing.

BASE CAMERA (3D World)
  - Clear Flags: Color & Depth
  - Render Layer: Everything minus UI

OVERLAY CAMERA (Selection Highlights)
  - Clear Flags: Nothing (Depth Only)
  - Render Layer: "Selected"
  - Effect: Glowing Bloom

OVERLAY CAMERA (UI)
  - Clear Flags: Nothing
  - Render Layer: UI

This ensures your UI always draws on top of the world, and your highlighting effects don't get messed up by the world's depth buffer if you don't want them to.

Key Takeaways

  1. Shadows are relative. Scale requires switching between high-quality cascades for close-ups and persistent depth maps for distance.
  2. Stencil Buffers are your best tool for region-based masking (highlighting specific zones).
  3. Depth Testing (ZTest) can be subverted for visibility effects like X-Ray.
  4. Camera Stacking is the architectural way to keep UI, highlights, and world geography from interfering with each other's render states.
